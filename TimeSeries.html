Time Series & Models — AR, MA, ARMA, ARIMA

1. What is a Time Series?

A time series is a sequence of observations recorded over time (usually at equal intervals): daily, monthly, yearly, etc.

Example

YearSales
20201000
20211200
20221300
20231250
20241400

2. Components of a Time Series

Most time series can be decomposed into:

ComponentMeaningExample
Trend (T)Long-term increase or decreaseSales rising over years
Seasonal (S)Repeating pattern over a fixed periodHigher sales every December
Cyclical (C)Long-term ups & downs (irregular period)Business cycles over several years
Irregular (I)Random/noiseSudden drop due to strikes

Compact model form:

Yt = Tt + St + Ct + It

3. Time Series Models

When forecasting, we try to capture past patterns. The main statistical models are:

  • AR (Auto-Regressive)
  • MA (Moving Average)
  • ARMA (Auto-Regressive Moving Average)
  • ARIMA (Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average)

AR — Auto-Regressive Model

The current value depends on past values of the series.

Y_t = c + φ₁ Y_{t-1} + φ₂ Y_{t-2} + ... + φ_p Y_{t-p} + ε_t

Where:

  • Y_t = current value
  • φ_i = AR coefficients
  • ε_t = white noise (error)

Example (AR(1)):

Y_t = 0.6 Y_{t-1} + ε_t

MA — Moving Average Model

The current value depends on past error terms (shocks).

Y_t = μ + ε_t + θ₁ ε_{t-1} + θ₂ ε_{t-2} + ... + θ_q ε_{t-q}

Where θ_i are MA coefficients.

Example (MA(1)):

Y_t = μ + ε_t + 0.5 ε_{t-1}

ARMA — Combined Model

Used when the series is stationary. It combines AR and MA parts:

Y_t = c + φ₁ Y_{t-1} + ... + φ_p Y_{t-p} + ε_t + θ₁ ε_{t-1} + ... + θ_q ε_{t-q}

This models both dependence on past values and past shocks.

ARIMA — For Non-Stationary Series

ARIMA is used when the series has a trend or is non-stationary. The I stands for Integrated (differencing to make the series stationary):

ARIMA(p, d, q)

  • p: order of AR part
  • d: number of differences (integration)
  • q: order of MA part

Example workflow when series has an upward trend:

  1. Take first difference: Y'_t = Y_t - Y_{t-1}
  2. Fit an ARMA model on the differenced data.
(1 - φ₁ B - ... - φ_p B^p)(1 - B)^d Y_t = (1 + θ₁ B + ... + θ_q B^q) ε_t

(Here B is the backshift operator: B Y_t = Y_{t-1}.)

5. Intuitive Recap

  • AR: "Today depends on yesterday's result."
  • MA: "Today depends on yesterday's surprise (error)."
  • ARMA: "Today depends on both yesterday's result and surprise."
  • ARIMA: "If data trends, first remove trend via differencing, then use ARMA."
